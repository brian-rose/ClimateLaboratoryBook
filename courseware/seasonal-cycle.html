2. Analytical toy model of the seasonal cycle¶

What factors determine the above pattern of seasonal temperatures? How large is the winter-to-summer variation in temperature? What is its phasing relative to the seasonal variations in insolation?

We will start to examine this in a very simple zero-dimensional EBM.

Suppose the seasonal cycle of insolation at a point is

Q=Q∗sin⁡ωt+Q0Q = Q^* \sin\omega t + Q_0Q=Q∗sinωt+Q0​
(1)

where ω=2π year−1\omega = 2\pi ~ \text{year}^{-1}ω=2π year−1, Q0Q_0Q0​ is the annual mean insolation, and Q∗Q^*Q∗ is the amplitude of the seasonal variations.

Here ω t=0\omega ~ t=0ω t=0 is spring equinox, ω t=π/2\omega~t = \pi/2ω t=π/2 is summer solstice, ω t=π\omega~t = \piω t=π is fall equinox, and ω t=3π/2 \omega ~t = 3 \pi/2ω t=3π/2 is winter solstice.

Now suppose the temperature is governed by

CdTdt=Q−(A+B T)C \frac{d T}{d t} = Q - (A + B~T)CdtdT​=Q−(A+B T)
(2)

so that we have a simple model

CdTdt=Q∗sin⁡ωt+Q0−(A+B T)C \frac{d T}{d t} = Q^* \sin\omega t + Q_0 - (A + B~T)CdtdT​=Q∗sinωt+Q0​−(A+B T)
(3)

We want to ask two questions:

  1. What is the amplitude of the seasonal temperature variation?
  2. When does the temperature maximum occur?

We will look for an oscillating solution

T(t)=T0+T∗sin⁡(ωt−Φ)T(t) = T_0 + T^* \sin(\omega t - \Phi)T(t)=T0​+T∗sin(ωt−Φ)
(4)

where Φ\PhiΦ is an unknown phase shift and T∗T^*T∗ is the unknown amplitude of seasonal temperature variations.

The annual mean:¶

Integrate over one year to find

T‾=T0\overline{T} = T_0T=T0​
(5)
Q0=A+B T‾Q_0 = A + B ~ \overline{T}Q0​=A+B T
(6)

so that

T0=Q0−ABT_0 = \frac{Q_0 - A}{B}T0​=BQ0​−A​
(7)

The seasonal problem¶

Now we need to solve for T∗T^*T∗ and Φ\PhiΦ.

Take the derivative

dTdt=T∗ωcos⁡(ωt−Φ)\frac{d T}{dt} = T^* \omega \cos(\omega t - \Phi)dtdT​=T∗ωcos(ωt−Φ)
(8)

and plug into the model equation to get

C T∗ωcos⁡(ωt−Φ)=Q∗sin⁡ωt+Q0−(A+B (T0+T∗sin⁡(ωt−Φ)))\begin{align*} C~ T^* \omega \cos(\omega t - \Phi) &= Q^* \sin\omega t + Q_0 \\ & - \left( A + B~(T_0 + T^* \sin(\omega t - \Phi) )\right) \end{align*}C T∗ωcos(ωt−Φ)​=Q∗sinωt+Q0​−(A+B (T0​+T∗sin(ωt−Φ)))​
(9)

Subtracting out the annual mean leaves us with

C T∗ωcos⁡(ωt−Φ)=Q∗sin⁡ωt−B T∗sin⁡(ωt−Φ)C~ T^* \omega \cos(\omega t - \Phi) = Q^* \sin\omega t - B ~ T^* \sin(\omega t - \Phi)C T∗ωcos(ωt−Φ)=Q∗sinωt−B T∗sin(ωt−Φ)
(10)

Zero heat capacity: the radiative equilibrium solution¶

It’s instructive to first look at the case with C=0C=0C=0, which means that the system is not capable of storing heat, and the temperature must always be in radiative equilibrium with the insolation.

In this case we would have

Q∗sin⁡ωt=B T∗sin⁡(ωt−Φ)Q^* \sin\omega t = B ~ T^* \sin(\omega t - \Phi)Q∗sinωt=B T∗sin(ωt−Φ)
(11)

which requires that the phase shift is

Φ=0\Phi = 0Φ=0
(12)

and the amplitude is

T∗=Q∗BT^* = \frac{Q^*}{B}T∗=BQ∗​
(13)

With no heat capacity, there can be no phase shift! The temperature goes up and does in lockstep with the insolation. As we will see, the amplitude of the temperature variations is maximum in this limit.

As a practical example: at 45ºN the amplitude of the seasonal insolation cycle is about 180 W m−2^{-2}−2 (see the Insolation notes -- the difference between insolation at summer and winter solstice is about 360 W m−2^{-2}−2 which we divide by two to get the amplitude of seasonal variations).

We will follow our previous EBM work and take B=2B = 2B=2 W m−2^{-2}−2 K−1^{-1}−1. This would give a seasonal temperature amplitude of 90ºC!

This highlights to important role for heat capacity to buffer the seasonal variations in sunlight.

Non-dimensional heat capacity parameter¶

We can rearrange the seasonal equation to give

C ωBcos⁡(ωt−Φ)+sin⁡(ωt−Φ)=Q∗B T∗sin⁡ωt\frac{C~\omega}{B} \cos(\omega t - \Phi) + \sin(\omega t - \Phi) = \frac{Q^*}{B~T^*} \sin\omega tBC ω​cos(ωt−Φ)+sin(ωt−Φ)=B T∗Q∗​sinωt
(14)

The heat capacity appears in our equation through the non-dimensional ratio

C~=C ωB\tilde{C} = \frac{C~\omega}{B}C~=BC ω​
(15)

This parameter measures the efficiency of heat storage versus damping of energy anomalies through longwave radiation to space in our system.

We will now use trigonometric identities

cos⁡(ωt−Φ)=cos⁡ωtcos⁡Φ+sin⁡ωtsin⁡Φsin⁡(ωt−Φ)=sin⁡ωtcos⁡Φ−cos⁡ωtsin⁡Φ\begin{align*} \cos(\omega t - \Phi) &= \cos\omega t \cos\Phi + \sin\omega t \sin\Phi \\ \sin(\omega t - \Phi) &= \sin\omega t \cos\Phi - \cos\omega t \sin\Phi \end{align*}cos(ωt−Φ)sin(ωt−Φ)​=cosωtcosΦ+sinωtsinΦ=sinωtcosΦ−cosωtsinΦ​
(16)

to express our equation as

Q∗B T∗sin⁡ωt=C~cos⁡ωtcos⁡Φ+C~sin⁡ωtsin⁡Φ+sin⁡ωtcos⁡Φ−cos⁡ωtsin⁡Φ\begin{align*} \frac{Q^*}{B~T^*} \sin\omega t = &\tilde{C} \cos\omega t \cos\Phi \\ + &\tilde{C} \sin\omega t \sin\Phi \\ + &\sin\omega t \cos\Phi \\ - &\cos\omega t \sin\Phi \end{align*}B T∗Q∗​sinωt=++−​C~cosωtcosΦC~sinωtsinΦsinωtcosΦcosωtsinΦ​
(17)

Now gathering together all terms in cos⁡ωt\cos\omega tcosωt and sin⁡ωt\sin\omega tsinωt:

cos⁡ωt(C~cos⁡Φ−sin⁡Φ)=sin⁡ωt(Q∗B T∗−C~sin⁡Φ−cos⁡Φ)\cos\omega t \left( \tilde{C} \cos\Phi - \sin\Phi \right) = \sin\omega t \left( \frac{Q^*}{B~T^*} - \tilde{C} \sin\Phi - \cos\Phi \right)cosωt(C~cosΦ−sinΦ)=sinωt(B T∗Q∗​−C~sinΦ−cosΦ)
(18)

Solving for the phase shift¶

The equation above must be true for all ttt, which means that sum of terms in each set of parentheses must be zero.

We therefore have an equation for the phase shift

C~cos⁡Φ−sin⁡Φ=0\tilde{C} \cos\Phi - \sin\Phi = 0C~cosΦ−sinΦ=0
(19)

which means that the phase shift is

Φ=arctan⁡C~\Phi = \arctan \tilde{C}Φ=arctanC~
(20)

Solving for the amplitude¶

The other equation is

Q∗B T∗−C~sin⁡Φ−cos⁡Φ=0\frac{Q^*}{B~T^*} - \tilde{C} \sin\Phi - \cos\Phi = 0B T∗Q∗​−C~sinΦ−cosΦ=0
(21)

or

Q∗B T∗−cos⁡Φ(1+C~2)=0\frac{Q^*}{B~T^*} - \cos\Phi \left( 1+ \tilde{C}^2 \right) = 0B T∗Q∗​−cosΦ(1+C~2)=0
(22)

which we solve for T∗T^*T∗ to get

T∗=Q∗B1(1+C~2)cos⁡(arctan⁡C~)T^* = \frac{Q^*}{B} \frac{1}{\left( 1+ \tilde{C}^2 \right) \cos\left(\arctan \tilde{C} \right) }T∗=BQ∗​(1+C~2)cos(arctanC~)1​
(23)

Shallow water limit:¶

In low heat capacity limit,

C~<<1\tilde{C} << 1C~<<1
(24)

the phase shift is

Φ≈C~\Phi \approx \tilde{C}Φ≈C~
(25)

and the amplitude is

T∗=Q∗B(1−C~)T^* = \frac{Q^*}{B} \left( 1 - \tilde{C} \right)T∗=BQ∗​(1−C~)
(26)

Notice that for a system with very little heat capacity, the phase shift approaches zero and the amplitude approaches its maximum value T∗=Q∗BT^* = \frac{Q^*}{B}T∗=BQ∗​.

In the shallow water limit the temperature maximum will occur just slightly after the insolation maximum, and the seasonal temperature variations will be large.

Deep water limit:¶

Suppose instead we have an infinitely large heat reservoir (e.g. very deep ocean mixed layer).

In the limit C~→∞\tilde{C} \rightarrow \inftyC~→∞, the phase shift tends toward

Φ→π2\Phi \rightarrow \frac{\pi}{2}Φ→2π​
(27)

so the warming is nearly perfectly out of phase with the insolation -- peak temperature would occur at fall equinox.

But the amplitude in this limit is very small!

T∗→0T^* \rightarrow 0T∗→0
(28)

What values of C~\tilde{C}C~ are realistic?¶

We need to evaluate

C~=C ωB\tilde{C} = \frac{C~\omega}{B}C~=BC ω​
(29)

for reasonable values of CCC and BBB.

BBB is the longwave radiative feedback in our system: a measure of how efficiently a warm anomaly is radiated away to space. We have previously chosen B=2B = 2B=2 W m−2^{-2}−2 K−1^{-1}−1.

CCC is the heat capacity of the whole column, a number in J m−2^{-2}−2 K−1^{-1}−1.

Heat capacity of the atmosphere¶

Integrating from the surface to the top of the atmosphere, we can write

Ca=∫0pscpdpgC_a = \int_0^{p_s} c_p \frac{dp}{g}Ca​=∫0ps​​cp​gdp​
(30)

where cp=103c_p = 10^3cp​=103 J kg−1^{-1}−1 K−1^{-1}−1 is the specific heat at constant pressure for a unit mass of air, and dp/gdp/gdp/g is a mass element.

This gives Ca≈107C_a \approx 10^7Ca​≈107 J m−2^{-2}−2 K−1^{-1}−1.

Heat capacity of a water surface¶

As we wrote back in the notes on Modeling the Global Energy Budget, the heat capacity for a well-mixed column of water is

Cw=cwρwHwC_w = c_w \rho_w H_wCw​=cw​ρw​Hw​
(31)

where

cw=4×103c_w = 4 \times 10^3cw​=4×103 J kg−1^{-1}−1 °C−1^{-1}−1 is the specific heat of water,

ρw=103\rho_w = 10^3ρw​=103 kg m−3^{-3}−3 is the density of water, and

HwH_w Hw​ is the depth of the water column

The heat capacity of the entire atmosphere is thus equivalent to 2.5 meters of water.

C~\tilde{C}C~ for a dry land surface¶

A dry land surface has very little heat capacity and CCC is actually dominated by the atmosphere. So we can take C=Ca=107C = C_a = 10^7C=Ca​=107 J m−2^{-2}−2 K−1^{-1}−1 as a reasonable lower bound.

So our lower bound on C~\tilde{C}C~ is thus, taking B=2B = 2B=2 W m−2^{-2}−2 K−1^{-1}−1 and ω=2π year−1=2×10−7 s−1\omega = 2\pi ~ \text{year}^{-1} = 2 \times 10^{-7} \text{ s}^{-1}ω=2π year−1=2×10−7 s−1:

C~=1\tilde{C} = 1C~=1
(32)

C~\tilde{C}C~ for a 100 meter ocean mixed layer¶

Setting Hw=100H_w = 100Hw​=100 m gives Cw=4×108C_w = 4 \times 10^8Cw​=4×108 J m−2^{-2}−2 K−1^{-1}−1. Then our non-dimensional parameter is

C~=40\tilde{C} = 40C~=40
(33)

The upshot: C~\tilde{C}C~ is closer to the deep water limit¶

Even for a dry land surface, C~\tilde{C}C~ is not small. This means that there is always going to be a substantial phase shift in the timing of the peak temperatures, and a reduction in the seasonal amplitude.

The seasonal phase shift¶

Even for the driest surfaces the phase shift is about 45º and the amplitude is half of its theoretical maximum. For most wet surfaces the cycle is damped out and delayed further.

Of course we are already familiar with this phase shift from our day-to-day experience. Our calendar says that summer “begins” at the solstice and last until the equinox.

3. Exploring the amplitude of the seasonal cycle with an EBM¶

Something important is missing from this toy model: heat transport!

The amplitude of the seasonal cycle of insolation increases toward the poles, but the seasonal temperature variations are partly mitigated by heat transport from lower, warmer latitudes.

Our 1D diffusive EBM is the appropriate tool for exploring this further.

We are looking at the 1D (zonally averaged) energy balance model with diffusive heat transport. The equation is

C∂Ts∂t=(1−α) Q−(A+B Ts)+Dcos⁡⁡ϕ∂∂ϕ(cos⁡⁡ϕ ∂Ts∂ϕ)C \frac{\partial T_s}{\partial t} = (1-\alpha) ~ Q - \left( A + B~T_s \right) + \frac{D}{\cos⁡\phi } \frac{\partial }{\partial \phi} \left( \cos⁡\phi ~ \frac{\partial T_s}{\partial \phi} \right)C∂t∂Ts​​=(1−α) Q−(A+B Ts​)+cos⁡ϕD​∂ϕ∂​(cos⁡ϕ ∂ϕ∂Ts​​)
(34)

with the albedo given by

α(ϕ)=α0+α2P2(sin⁡ϕ)\alpha(\phi) = \alpha_0 + \alpha_2 P_2(\sin\phi)α(ϕ)=α0​+α2​P2​(sinϕ)
(35)

and we will use

climlab.EBM_seasonal

to solve this model numerically.

One handy feature of climlab process code: the function integrate_years() automatically calculates the time averaged temperature. So if we run it for exactly one year, we get the annual mean temperature (and many other diagnostics) saved in the dictionary timeave.

We will look at the seasonal cycle of temperature in three different models with different heat capacities (which we express through an equivalent depth of water in meters).

All other parameters will be as chosen in the notes on the one-dimensional energy balance model (which focussed on tuning the EBM to the annual mean energy budget).

4. The seasonal cycle for a planet with 90º obliquity¶

The EBM code uses our familiar insolation.py code to calculate insolation, and therefore it’s easy to set up a model with different orbital parameters. Here is an example with very different orbital parameters: 90º obliquity. We looked at the distribution of insolation by latitude and season for this type of planet in the last homework.

orb_highobl = {'ecc':0., 
               'obliquity':90., 
               'long_peri':0.}

Tann_highobl = np.empty( [lat.size, num_depths] )
models_highobl = []

for n in range(num_depths):
    model = climlab.EBM_seasonal(water_depth=water_depths[n],

    models_highobl.append(model)
    models_highobl[n].integrate_years(40., verbose=False )
    models_highobl[n].integrate_years(1., verbose=False)

Tyear_highobl = np.empty([lat.size, num_steps_per_year, num_depths])
for n in range(num_depths):

Why is the temperature so uniform in the north-south direction with 50 meters of water?

To see the reason, let’s plot the annual mean insolation at 90º obliquity, alongside the present-day annual mean insolation:

lat2 = np.linspace(-90, 90, 181)
days = np.linspace(1.,50.)/50 * const.days_per_year
Q_present = climlab.solar.insolation.daily_insolation( lat2, days )
Q_highobl = climlab.solar.insolation.daily_insolation( lat2, days, orb_highobl )
Q_present_ann = np.mean( Q_present, axis=1 )
Q_highobl_ann = np.mean( Q_highobl, axis=1 )
fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.plot( lat2, Q_present_ann, label='Earth' )
ax.plot( lat2, Q_highobl_ann, label='90deg obliquity' )
ax.grid()
ax.legend(loc='lower center')
ax.set_xlabel('Latitude', fontsize=14 )
ax.set_ylabel('W m$^{-2}$', fontsize=14 )
ax.set_title('Annual mean insolation for two different obliquities', fontsize=16)

Though this is a bit misleading, because our model prescribes an increase in albedo from the equator to the pole. So the absorbed shortwave gradients look even more different.
